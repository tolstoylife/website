Christian anarchism (Христианский анархизм) is a political philosophy that holds that the teachings of Jesus — particularly the Sermon on the Mount — are incompatible with the existence of the state, with coercive authority, and with the use of violence. [[Leo Tolstoy]]'s later writings are the most influential articulation of this position, though the term itself was not coined by him.

## Tolstoy and the label

The term "Christian anarchism" appears to have entered use through reviews of Tolstoy's *The Kingdom of God Is Within You* after its publication in 1894. Tolstoy never used the term about himself or his ideas.

He rejected the label "anarchist" on specific grounds: in the political context of the 1880s–1900s, anarchism was associated with political violence — bombings, assassinations, and revolutionary terror. To call oneself an anarchist was to imply sympathy with those methods, which Tolstoy's philosophy of non-resistance categorically excluded.

Yet his engagement with anarchist thought was substantive. In *On Anarchy* (1900), he acknowledged that anarchists were right in their critique of the existing order, but held that anarchy could not be achieved through revolution — only through a growing number of individuals who no longer require or submit to governmental power. He rejected "terrorism, dynamite bombs and daggers" as methods that provoke reaction and destroy the power of public opinion.

## Tolstoy's own terminology

Rather than adopting labels from political movements, Tolstoy described his position using his own terms drawn directly from the Gospels: "non-resistance to evil by violence" (from Matthew 5:39), "the Kingdom of God is within you" (Luke 17:21), and what he called "true Christianity" — the ethical core of Christ's teaching stripped of church dogma, miracles, and institutional authority.

This framing was deliberate: Tolstoy maintained that he was not proposing anything new, but recovering what had always been present in the Gospels. Naming his position after a political movement — even one he partially agreed with — would have placed it within a human ideology rather than, as he saw it, within eternal truth.

## Scholarly use

Modern scholarship routinely uses "Christian anarchism" or "Christian anarcho-pacifism" as a descriptive category for Tolstoy's political thought. Alexandre Christoyannopoulos's *Tolstoy's Political Thought: Christian Anarcho-Pacifist Iconoclasm Then and Now* (Routledge, 2009) is the most comprehensive treatment, and uses the label explicitly while acknowledging that Tolstoy himself avoided it.
