Vladimir Grigoryevich Chertkov (Владимир Григорьевич Чертков; 3 November 1854 NS – 9 November 1936) was the most influential disciple of [[Leo Tolstoy]], the principal leader of the Tolstoyan movement as a public phenomenon, and the editor responsible for bringing Tolstoy's later writings to international audiences.

Born into a wealthy aristocratic family, Chertkov met Tolstoy in 1883 and immediately became his devoted follower and collaborator. With Tolstoy's support, he founded the *Posrednik* ("Intermediary") publishing house in Moscow, which disseminated Tolstoy's moral and religious writings — including works banned by the censors — at low prices accessible to peasant readers.

Following exile from Russia by the Tsarist authorities (partly due to his role in publicising the plight of the Doukhobors), Chertkov established the Free Word press in England, which continued publishing Tolstoy's censored works abroad. He returned to Russia after the 1905 revolution.

Chertkov served as chief editor of the 90-volume Jubilee Edition of Tolstoy's Complete Works (*Полное собрание сочинений*, 1928–1964), the canonical scholarly edition. His control over Tolstoy's literary estate and his influence over the aging Tolstoy caused severe conflict with [[Sophia Tolstaya]] in the final years of Tolstoy's life. Tolstoy ultimately signed a secret will, prepared with Chertkov's assistance, that excluded his family from the copyright in his works.
