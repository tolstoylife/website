Tolstoyanism (Толстовство) is the term applied to the social, religious, and political movement that grew from [[Leo Tolstoy]]'s later writings — particularly his works on non-resistance, Christian ethics, and rejection of state authority. The movement attracted followers in Russia, Britain, and elsewhere from the mid-1880s onwards, and its adherents established communal settlements, published Tolstoy's banned works, and practised vegetarianism, manual labour, and conscientious objection.

Tolstoy himself rejected the label. In a letter to an adherent (exact date and Jubilee Edition volume not yet confirmed), he wrote that to speak of "Tolstoyism" and to seek his guidance was "a great and gross error," insisting that there was no teaching of his own — only the eternal teaching of truth as expressed in the Gospels. He urged followers to listen to their own conscience rather than to follow him, and he never associated with the Tolstoyan movement as an organisation.

The tension between the movement and its namesake is central to understanding Tolstoyanism: the philosophy it drew on — non-resistance to evil by violence, renunciation of property, direct engagement with the Gospels — was, in Tolstoy's view, not his invention but a rediscovery of what had always been present in Christianity. Naming the movement after him was, from his perspective, to misplace the emphasis.

Key figures in the movement include [[Vladimir Chertkov]], who became its most influential organiser and publisher; [[Pavel Birukoff]], who wrote one of the earliest comprehensive biographies; and Aylmer Maude, who translated Tolstoy's works and later distanced himself from the movement's more dogmatic adherents.
